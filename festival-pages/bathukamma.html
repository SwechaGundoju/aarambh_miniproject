Bathukamma Festival

History

Bathukamma is an ancient floral festival celebrated predominantly in Telangana. The word "Bathukamma" means 'Mother Goddess, come alive!' in Telugu. It honors Goddess Gauri (a form of Durga) and symbolizes life, fertility, and womanhood. According to legend, a Chola king's daughter, who was reborn after prayers to Goddess Lakshmi, was named Bathukamma. The festival is believed to have started as a way to worship nature and thank the Earth for its bounty. It is traditionally celebrated during Navratri and marks the return of women to their maternal homes. Over the centuries, Bathukamma has become a symbol of Telangana's cultural pride and unity.

Significance

Batukamma celebrates the inherent relationship between earth, water and the human beings. During the entire preceding week, women make ‘boddemma’ (a deity of Gowri – mother Durga – made with earthly mud) along with Batukamma and immerse it in the pond. This helps reinforce the ponds and helps it retain more water. The flowers used in Batukamma have a great quality of purifying water and such flowers when immersed in abundance into the pond have the effect of cleansing the water and making the environment much better. In times where the fresh water ponds are gradually diminishing and dwindling away it is indeed a pride of Telangana that its womenfolk (with mostly agrarian background) inherently know how to make them better by celebrating the beauty of nature. It is something we indeed have to feel proud of. The festival heralds the beauty of nature, collective spirit of Telangana people, the indomitable spirit of women folks and also the scientific approach of the agrarian people towards preserving the resources of nature in a celebrative way. Hence, Bathukamma is the icon of cultural identity of Telangana.

Rituals and Traditions

Women create flower stacks using native blooms like thangedu, gunuka, banti, and chamanti. These are arranged in a tapering, temple-like structure. Dressed in traditional sarees, women sing folk songs and dance around the Bathukamma. On the final day, the arrangements are immersed in lakes or rivers with prayers for health and happiness. Bathukamma is a hindu flower-festival celebrated by the women of Telangana and some parts of Andhra Pradesh. Every year this festival is celebrated as per the Sathavahana calendar for nine days starting on Pitru Amavasya, which usually coincides with the months September–October of the Gregorian calendar. Bathukamma is celebrated for nine days and corresponds to the festivals of Sharad Navratri and Durga Puja. It starts on the day of Mahalaya Amavasya and the 9-day festivities culminate on "Saddula Bathukamma" or "Pedda Bathukamma." Bathukamma is followed by Boddemma, which is a 7-day festival. The Boddemma festival that marks the ending of Varsha Ruthu whereas Bathukamma marks the beginning of Sarad or Sharath Ruthu.

9 Nights of Bathukamma

  • Day 1: Engili Pula Bathukamma
  • Day 2: Atukula Bathukamma
  • Day 3: Muddapappu Bathukamma
  • Day 4: Nanabiyyam Bathukamma
  • Day 5: Atla Bathukamma
  • Day 6: Aligina Bathukamma (Alaka Bathukamma)
  • Day 7: Vepakayala Bathukamma
  • Day 8: Vennela Muddala Bathukamma
  • Day 9: Saddula Bathukamma
Engili Pula Bathukamma (Mahalaya Amavasya / Pethara Amavasya)
Naivedyam: Sesame seeds (Nuvvulu) with rice flour (Biyyampindi) or coarsely ground wet rice (Nookalu).
Atukula Bathukamma (Padyami – 1st day of Ashwayuja Masam)
Naivedyam: Flattened rice (Atkulu) with boiled lentils (Sappidi Pappu) and jaggery (Bellam).
Muddapappu Bathukamma (Vidiya – 2nd day)
Naivedyam: Soft boiled lentils (Muddapappu), milk, and jaggery.
Nanabiyyam Bathukamma (Thidiya – 3rd day)
Naivedyam: Soaked rice (Nanabiyyam), milk, and jaggery.
Atla Bathukamma (Chaturthi – 4th day)
Naivedyam: Dosas or pan cakes made from wheatlets (Uppidi Pindi Atlu).
Aligina Bathukamma / Alaka Bathukamma(Panchami – 5th day)
No Naivedyam: This day marks a break in the offerings.
Vepakayala Bathukamma (Shashti – 6th day)
Naivedyam: Rice flour balls shaped like neem fruits and deep-fried.
Vennamuddala Bathukamma (Saptami – 7th day)
Naivedyam: Butter or ghee with sesame seeds (Nuvvulu) and jaggery.
Saddula Bathukamma (Ashtami – 8th day / Durgashtami)
Naivedyam:
Five types of rice dishes:
  • Curd rice (Perugannam Saddi)
  • Tamarind rice (Pulihora Saddi)
  • Lemon rice (Nimmakaya Saddi)
  • Coconut rice (Kobbari Saddi)
  • Sesame rice (Nuvvula Saddi)
Saddula Bathukamma – Final Day Celebration On the 9th day (Durgashtami), Bathukamma is immersed in local water bodies with women dressed in traditional attire, singing and dancing to folk beats. Before immersion, Gauramma, a turmeric idol representing Goddess Parvati, is retrieved and the turmeric paste is applied to the Mangalsutra by married women for the well-being of their husbands. The atmosphere is filled with devotion, joy, and colorful floral arrangements floating on water. Maleeda – Special Offering Each day a unique Naivedyam (offering) is prepared using ingredients like corn, sorghum, bajra, grams, jaggery, and milk. On the final day, a special sweet dish called Maleeda—made from jaggery and roti—is distributed among participants.

Where It Is Celebrated

Bathukamma is predominantly celebrated in the Indian state of Telangana, especially by women. The festival holds great cultural and religious significance for the people of Telangana, symbolizing the strength and spirit of womanhood. It is widely observed in cities like Hyderabad, Warangal, Karimnagar, and Nizamabad. Bathukamma celebrations have also spread to neighboring states like Andhra Pradesh. In recent years, Telangana communities across the globe also celebrate Bathukamma with great enthusiasm.
